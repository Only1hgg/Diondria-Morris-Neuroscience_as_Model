**Module 07 — Attention and Consciousness**

The brain receives 11 million bits of sensory information per second and consciously processes about 50 of them. Attention is the filter that determines which 50 win. Module 07 broke that filter down into three distinct networks identified by Michael Posner. The alerting network puts the brain on high alert through norepinephrine signaling from the brainstem. The orienting network shifts the attention spotlight from one location to another through the parietal cortex and superior colliculus. The executive network, anchored in the prefrontal cortex and anterior cingulate cortex, handles conflict resolution, impulse suppression, and voluntary focus. These three networks operate through fundamentally different mechanisms and can be selectively damaged independently, which is how clinical cases like hemispatial neglect — where damage to the right parietal cortex causes patients to ignore the entire left side of their world — revealed the orienting network's role in carving out the attentional spotlight. The module also introduced the distinction between bottom-up attention, where a stimulus grabs processing resources automatically, and top-down attention, where a prior goal directs focus deliberately. Both are necessary. Neither alone is sufficient.

The transformer's self-attention mechanism was examined as an AI parallel to this biological system, and the mapping is instructive precisely because it is partial. Softmax competition across attention weights mirrors the biased competition model where neural groups compete for processing resources and winners get amplified while losers get suppressed. Multi-head attention runs multiple spotlight patterns in parallel, resembling the brain's simultaneous engagement of spatial, feature, and object attention through separate circuits. The attention heatmap experiment in the lab made this visible directly. A pre-trained BERT model visualized across layers showed Layer 1 attending locally to immediate neighbors while Layer 11 showed global semantic integration, resolving the pronoun "it" back to "cat" across several tokens. That local-to-global progression mirrors the visual hierarchy from V1 to inferotemporal cortex covered in Module 05. The transformer arrives at a functionally similar solution through processing depth rather than anatomically distinct cortical regions.

The ChatGPT audit exposed the gaps cleanly across four experiments. The buried instruction test confirmed executive attention — ChatGPT answered the capital of Australia before addressing a birthday party because an explicit directive overrode the dominant topic. The prompt injection test showed resistance to inattentional blindness. It caught the pizza haiku instruction buried in a business report and refused it while completing the full analysis, the opposite of what the invisible gorilla experiment predicts for humans under focused attention. The cocktail party test was the most telling failure. ChatGPT was introduced to a user named Jordan, then asked to summarize an article containing one sentence referencing Jordan by name. It processed that sentence as neutral content. No relevance spike fired. No background monitor exists. The consciousness question sat underneath all of it. Module 07 argues that attention and consciousness are related but separable. You can have attention without consciousness, as subliminal priming demonstrates. ChatGPT is the clearest available example of that separation. Functional attention with no evidence of awareness behind it.
