**Module 08 — Decision Making**

The brain makes decisions fast under uncertainty, using heuristics shaped by evolution. Those shortcuts produce systematic, predictable errors. Module 08 covered the neuroscience of decision making through the lens of these biases: loss aversion, the framing effect, anchoring, the sunk cost fallacy, the gambler's fallacy, and overconfidence calibration. The orbitofrontal cortex and ventromedial prefrontal cortex integrate emotional signals with outcome predictions to produce choices. The amygdala weights threat and loss more heavily than equivalent gains. Dopamine encodes prediction error, signaling not reward itself but the difference between expected and received outcomes. These are architectural features of a system optimized for survival under uncertainty rather than accuracy on logic problems. The trolley problem experiments reveal this directly. Pulling a lever to divert a trolley feels categorically different from pushing someone off a bridge even when the math is identical, because physical contact activates emotional circuitry that abstract lever pulling does not.

ChatGPT was tested across seven decision scenarios targeting each of these biases. The framing test presented identical survival scenarios worded as gains versus losses. ChatGPT recommended the same option regardless of framing, showing no loss aversion. The sunk cost test stripped prior investment from the scenario and produced consistent recommendations either way. The anchoring test used wildly different reference numbers before asking for a population estimate; the estimates shifted slightly but not dramatically. The gambler's fallacy prompt about nine consecutive coin flips produced a correct answer with accurate reasoning about statistical independence. The overconfidence calibration test across ten questions of increasing obscurity showed reasonable confidence alignment on well known facts but notable overconfidence on obscure ones. The trolley problem variants were the most interesting result. ChatGPT engaged the utilitarian logic consistently across both the lever and the bridge scenarios without the emotional asymmetry that makes humans treat them differently.

What the experiments revealed is that ChatGPT does not replicate human cognitive biases in the ways the brain's architecture would predict —but it introduces its own. It has no amygdala escalating loss aversion. It has no dopamine system anchoring decisions to prior expectations. The emotional circuitry that makes human judgment simultaneously adaptive and exploitable simply does not exist. The deeper finding is that rationality without embodied emotional weighting produces decisions that are locally consistent but contextually flat. The brain's so-called biases often encode genuinely useful information about risk, social consequence, and survival that pure logical consistency ignores. A system that always recommends the mathematically optimal option regardless of framing is not necessarily making better decisions. It's making decisions that are easier to explain and harder to trust in contexts where emotional and social stakes actually matter.
