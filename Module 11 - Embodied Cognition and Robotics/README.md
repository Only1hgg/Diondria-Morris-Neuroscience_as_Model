**Module 11 — Embodied Cognition and Robotics**

Intelligence does not live in the brain alone. Two thirds of an octopus's neurons are distributed across its arms. Slime mold solves maze problems without any brain at all. Module 11 opened with these examples to make a point that reframes everything covered in the course: cognition is a property of an entire organism interacting with its environment, not a property of a computational unit sitting inside a body that happens to have sensors. The cerebellum alone contains roughly 80% of all the brain's neurons despite occupying only 10% of its volume, and its entire function is a predict-act-correct loop. The motor cortex generates a movement plan, the cerebellum predicts the sensory feedback that should arrive, compares it against what actually comes in, and sends correction signals when they diverge. The CMAC algorithm developed by James Albus in the 1970s modeled this loop directly and became one of the first successful machine learning applications in real robotic systems, decades before deep learning made prediction-correction architectures mainstream. Proprioception adds another layer: muscle spindles, Golgi tendon organs, and joint receptors feeding the brain a continuous stream of body position data that most people never consciously notice until it fails. Modern robots have joint encoders that approximate a fraction of this capability, which is a primary reason robotic manipulation remains far behind human manipulation even in controlled environments.

The module's core challenge to AI is not about processing power or architecture. It is about the sensorimotor loop. A language model receives digital input and produces digital output with no physical presence, no consequence for being wrong in the world, and no body to act through. The efference copy system — the brain sends a simultaneous copy of every motor command to sensory regions to predict and cancel expected feedback has no equivalent in any deployed AI system. The rubber hand illusion demonstrates that body ownership itself is a constructed model the brain maintains and updates continuously, not a fixed hardware readout. Building AI that operates in the physical world requires solving proprioception, interoception, efference copies, and the full perception-action loop as engineering problems, not just as theoretical algorithms.
