**Module 02 — Foundations of Neuroscience**

The brain does not experience reality. It constructs. That's a mechanistic description of what sensory neurons actually deliver. Raw electrical spikes with no labels and no meaning arrive at the cortex, and somewhere in that process, objects, colors, spatial relationships, and unified conscious experience emerge. Module 02 asked how that happens and what the underlying computational architecture looks like. The answer introduced four major theories: the Thousand Brains Theory, the Free Energy Principle, the Bayesian Brain Hypothesis, and Global Workspace Theory. Each targeting a different piece of the same puzzle. None of them alone is the complete story. Together they describe a brain that predicts constantly, updates probabilistically, distributes processing across thousands of semi-independent units, and broadcasts winning interpretations to the rest of the system.

The Thousand Brains Theory was the anchor of this module and the direct inspiration for the lab. Jeff Hawkins proposes that the neocortex contains approximately 150,000 cortical columns, each roughly 0.5mm wide and housing around 100,000 neurons. The critical insight is that each column does not learn isolated features — it learns complete models of objects using reference frames similar to GPS coordinates for concepts. A single column touching a coffee cup can gradually learn the entire shape of that cup. Columns then communicate laterally, voting to reach consensus on the most likely interpretation of the current sensory input. This distributed setup is why the brain remains functional even when regions are damaged. No single column holds the answer. The answer emerges from agreement across many independent models, and that architecture has direct implications for how we think about fault tolerance and robustness in AI systems.

The lab translated this theory into working code. A cortical column voting simulation was built from scratch in Python, implementing sensory evidence matrices, a voting mechanism where columns share beliefs with neighbors, and a consensus detection system that tracks when column agreement crosses a defined threshold. The simulation ran experiments across varying noise levels, column counts, and vote weight parameters to test exactly the kinds of variables that matter in the biological system. High noise flattened confidence and made early consensus misleading. More columns produced modest accuracy gains through averaging. The vote weight experiments revealed the core trade-off the theory describes: columns that ignore their neighbors lose the benefit of distributed processing, while columns that abandon their own sensory evidence become vulnerable to majority error. The lab made that trade-off visible in data rather than just in theory.

What Module 02 established that carries through the rest of the course is the idea that the brain's most important properties are architectural, not just algorithmic. Predictive coding, free energy minimization, Bayesian belief updating. The parallel to modern AI is direct and uncomfortable. Neural networks today do not use reference frames. They do not distribute complete object models across semi-independent units that vote. They do not minimize prediction error through a hierarchy of top-down predictions and bottom-up corrections in the way Friston describes. They approximate some of these behaviors at the surface while missing the underlying architecture entirely.
