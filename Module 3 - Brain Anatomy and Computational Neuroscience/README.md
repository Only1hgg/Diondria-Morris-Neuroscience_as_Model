**Module 03 — Brain Anatomy and Computational Neuroscience**

The brain is a precisely organized system of specialized regions, each with defined roles, defined connectivity patterns, and defined failure modes when damaged. Module 03 built the anatomical foundation that every subsequent module depends on. Starting with macroscopic structure: lobes, regions, and the circuits that connect them — and moving down to the individual neuron, this module established the vocabulary and the spatial logic of biological intelligence. The amygdala sits near the brainstem, almond-shaped, responsible for emotional processing, memory modulation, face recognition, and decision-making. The prefrontal cortex handles executive function and planning. The hippocampus converts short-term experience into long-term memory and constructs spatial maps. These are not isolated facts. They are nodes in a network, and the connectivity between them is as important as the regions themselves. The amygdala-prefrontal cortex circuit alone governs how emotional states modulate rational judgment, a relationship with direct consequences for understanding why AI systems that lack any equivalent produce decisions that feel contextually flat.

Moving from anatomy to the neuron itself, this module introduced the functional unit of everything. Physical characteristics, electrophysiological properties, and spike patterns vary meaningfully across neuron types, and those differences matter computationally. The Hodgkin-Huxley model, developed in 1952, described spike propagation through four differential equations governing voltages and currents across the cell membrane. Izhikevich's 2004 work extended this into biologically realistic simulations of neuron populations, making it possible to model how neurons excite and suppress one another in the patterns that produce cognition. The course used Wolfram's neuroscience computational tools to explore these properties directly — querying neuron characteristics, visualizing axonal arborization in specific hippocampal subregions, and comparing spike patterns across cell types. Seeing electrophysiological data for hippocampal CA1 pyramidal cells alongside simulated equivalents made the gap between biological neurons and artificial ones concrete rather than abstract.

Brain regions associated with memory, decision-making, language, emotional processing, and visual perception are not randomly distributed. They reflect an architecture shaped by evolutionary pressure toward efficiency, with spatial proximity often reflecting functional relationship. Visual perception alone involves a hierarchical subprocess that categorizes faces, places, words, and numbers through distinctive activation patterns across the occipital and temporal lobes. The fMRI-based statistical maps available through Wolfram's AnatomicalFunctionalConcept queries made this organization queryable and visualizable in ways that turned anatomical knowledge from memorization into analysis. Understanding which cortical areas activate together, which circuits carry feedback versus feedforward signals, and how minimum cost flow moves between the amygdala and spinal cord are the kinds of structural questions that computational neuroscience is uniquely positioned to answer.

What this module installed, beyond the anatomy itself, is a way of reading the brain as an engineering document. Every structural feature has a functional rationale. The feedback loops between brain regions are not incidental. They are the mechanism by which the system corrects itself in real time. The specialization of cortical areas reflects a solution to the problem of processing diverse information streams simultaneously without interference. The layered organization of the neocortex, the separation of sensory and motor pathways, the convergence zones where multimodal information integrates. All of it reflects design logic that modern AI architectures have only partially and unevenly replicated. Module 03 made it possible to look at a neural network diagram and see not just an engineering schematic but a simplified echo of something far more intricate.
