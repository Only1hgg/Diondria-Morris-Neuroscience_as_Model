##Module 01 — Introduction to Neuroscience
Before this course, the connection between brain biology and artificial intelligence might have seemed like a loose metaphor; something professors say to make neural networks sound more interesting than they are. Module 01 dismantled that assumption immediately. The brain is not a metaphor for AI. It is a existence proof that general intelligence is physically possible, and it has been running a working implementation for hundreds of millions of years. This module opened with a question that reframes the entire field: instead of asking how to make transformers bigger, what can we actually learn by studying how biological intelligence works? The answer turns out to be a lot, starting with three assumptions that modern AI accepts without question and the brain violates completely.

The first assumption is that intelligence operates in discrete sequential steps. Standard neural networks pass data through defined layers in order. The brain runs as a continuous, parallel, asynchronous system where signals loop back, neurons fire at different times, and there is no clean separation between input and output. The second assumption is that learning and deployment are separate phases. Every major AI pipeline follows the same structure: collect data, train, freeze, deploy. Your brain does not have a deployment phase. It was learning as you read the last sentence. The third assumption is that dense computation is normal. That every parameter should activate on every forward pass. The brain fires only 1 to 10 percent of its neurons at any given moment. The rest are silent. That is not a limitation. That is the design. Sparse, selective computation is what allows a 20-watt biological system to outperform data centers on tasks that actually matter in the real world.

The 20-watt figure is not just a fun fact to cite in conversation. It is the central design constraint that explains why the brain is organized the way it is. Energy scarcity forces selectivity. When you cannot afford to process everything, you develop attention. When you cannot afford to store everything, you develop memory consolidation. When you cannot afford to relearn from scratch, you develop priors. Every elegant property of biological intelligence traces back to the pressure of operating under severe resource constraints. Modern AI was built in the opposite environment: abundant compute, abundant data, abundant energy and the architecture reflects that. This module made the case that any theory of intelligence which ignores energy as a design constraint is incomplete, and any AI system built for the real world rather than a data center will eventually have to reckon with what the brain figured out a long time ago.

What makes Module 01 relevant beyond the classroom is the mindset it installs. The dominant pattern in AI research is convergence, transformers worked, so everyone builds transformers. That success driven narrowing has its costs. It crowds out fundamentally different approaches before they get a fair hearing, and it makes the field slower to recognize when an underlying assumption is wrong. RNNs were the standard for sequences until attention mechanisms revealed a better framing entirely. Neuroscience functions as a corrective to that tunnel vision. It expands the design space by showing what a completely different organizing principle looks like when it actually works at scale. This module did not argue that AI should copy the brain. It argued that understanding the brain gives engineers a broader vocabulary for thinking about what intelligence can be and that is a competitive advantage worth having.
