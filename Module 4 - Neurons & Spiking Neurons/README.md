**Module 04 — Neurons and Spiking Neural Networks**

The neuron is a dynamic electrical system that integrates inputs over time, leaks charge continuously, and fires only when accumulated voltage crosses a threshold. Module 04 built a working implementation of that behavior from scratch using the Leaky Integrate-and-Fire model, the simplest computational framework that still captures the essential logic of biological spiking. The LIF equation describes membrane potential as a tug of war between incoming current pushing voltage upward and a leak term pulling it back toward resting potential at a rate governed by the membrane time constant tau. When voltage crosses the threshold of -55 mV, the neuron fires, resets below resting potential to simulate hyperpolarization, and the cycle begins again. The lab implemented this in a full simulation loop across 1000 time steps, producing 10 spikes under a constant current injection between 200ms and 800ms. Visualizing the membrane potential trace made the leaky integration tangible. Voltage curves upward between spikes rather than climbing linearly, slows as it approaches threshold, and drifts back toward rest after the current cuts off.

The second half of the lab addressed spike encoding, the process of converting conventional data formats into the spike trains that spiking neural networks actually consume. Rate coding treats each input value as a probability. Apixel intensity of 0.9 generates a spike at 90% of time steps, producing dense trains for high values and sparse trains for low ones. Latency coding encodes information in timing rather than frequency, with high-value inputs firing early and low-value inputs firing late or not at all. Delta modulation, the most biologically realistic of the three, generates spikes only when input changes beyond a threshold, mirroring how retinal photoreceptors respond to change rather than steady illumination. Each strategy represents a different philosophy about what information matters and how to represent it temporally, and the choice between them affects network performance in ways that have direct parallels in how different sensory systems in the brain prioritize different signal properties.

What makes spiking neural networks worth understanding is the efficiency argument. A conventional CNN with 10 million neurons activates every parameter on every forward pass. An equivalent SNN running across 25 time steps with 3% neuron activity at each step produces 7.5 million total spike events compared to 10 million activations and that difference widens as network size grows. More importantly, spiking networks are driven by events. Computation only happens when a spike occurs. Silent neurons cost nothing. This is the same principle that makes the brain's sparse computation architecture so effective, and it is why neuromorphic hardware like Intel's Loihi is designed around spike-based processing rather than the dense matrix operations that GPUs optimize for. The LIF neuron is the building block of a fundamentally different computational paradigm and Module 04 built it by hand to make sure that point landed.
