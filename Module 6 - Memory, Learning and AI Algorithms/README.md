**Module 06 — Memory, Learning and AI Algorithms**

The brain has four memory systems and they operate through different mechanisms in different regions for different purposes. Working memory holds roughly four items at a time through active firing in the prefrontal cortex. Episodic memory encodes specific experiences with temporal and spatial context through the hippocampus. Semantic memory stores generalized factual knowledge distributed across the neocortex. Procedural memory encodes motor skills and habits through the basal ganglia and cerebellum. Complementary Learning Systems theory ties these together by explaining why the hippocampus and neocortex operate on different timescales. The hippocampus binds new information fast from single exposures while the neocortex integrates knowledge slowly across massive experience. The hippocampus consolidates memories during sleep through replay, which is why the Ebbinghaus forgetting curve steepens without rest and why sleep deprivation produces measurable hippocampal atrophy over time. Each of these systems has a direct and imperfect analog in modern AI, and the gaps between biological memory and machine memory are not minor implementation details. They reflect fundamentally different relationships with time, experience, and consolidation.

ChatGPT was audited directly against these four memory systems across three experiments. The working memory test pushed the system to 50 items with no errors, far exceeding the brain's four-item biological limit but the mechanism is architectural, not cognitive. Everything within the context window remains equally accessible regardless of recency. The interference test introduced 20 unrelated topic exchanges between encoding and recall. ChatGPT retrieved both targets correctly, demonstrating that it does not forget through interference the way the brain does. Human forgetting is competitive. New information overwrites old during consolidation. ChatGPT's forgetting is structural. Information simply falls outside the context window with no consolidation process involved. The few-shot learning test used a fictional language called Zorblat to probe in-context pattern matching. ChatGPT applied the provided vocabulary correctly but retained nothing after the session ended. No synaptic change occurred and no hippocampal binding took place.

The audit placed ChatGPT firmly on the neocortex side of CLS theory. Its training is slow, massive, and produces stable generalized knowledge that does not update through experience. The hippocampal side is entirely absent; no fast binding, no sleep-dependent replay, no mechanism for a single conversation to leave a lasting trace. DeepMind's DQN used experience replay as a direct engineering analog to hippocampal consolidation, storing and replaying past experiences to stabilize learning. ChatGPT has no equivalent process. What this module made clear is that the memory limitations of current large language models are not engineering oversights waiting to be patched. They reflect a fundamental architectural choice to build on the neocortical side of the biological system while leaving the hippocampal side unaddressed and until that changes, no amount of scaling will produce a system that learns the way a brain learns.
